Easy Beauty by Chloé Cooper Jones


My rating: 5 of 5 stars

Easy Beauty is not an easy read, but, boy, is it worth it.

Chloe Cooper Jones tells her story of disability and motherhood as she copes with an unexpected pregnancy, navigates academia, and rehashes her difficult relationship with her father. Jones explores these complex themes not just through her own personal experience but also through a challenging lens of philosophy, even venturing into her own fraught relationship with philosophy itself. Nightcrawling by Leila Mottley


My rating: 5 of 5 stars

Nightcrawling is a stunning novel, combining powerful prose and a heart wrenching tale of poverty, race, and abuse. But it is also a story of family and friendship, of adolescence and first love.

The story is gritty, but the prose are smooth, if a little dense at times. But for a debut novel, Nightcrawling is complex and beautiful and necessary. I can't wait to see what Motley writes next. Read more here.

Her Body and Other Parties: Stories by Carmen Maria Machado


My rating: 4 of 5 stars

Haunting and compelling. I found many of these stories to be page turning and stomach churning. The writing ranges from conversational, fantastical narrative to poetic, fractured introspection. Any collection with so much diversity in approach will inevitably have some stories that work for some readers more than others. I found "Especially Heinous" to be compelling, if a little cartoonish, while I know others found it tedious. The tone and general argument bound the stories together nicely, granted my reading was informed by Machado's memoir In the Dream House, so I entered the collection with an anchoring notion of narrative background. Overall, I found the grotesque insight into femininity and sexuality and violence to be compelling and well explored.

Read more here.

The Right to Sex: Feminism in the Twenty-First Century by Amia Srinivasan


My rating: 5 of 5 stars

Great roadmap to understanding contemporary feminist issues. The essays are meticulously constructed, and I often had the feeling that the essay was reading my mind, answering my questions immediately after they popped into my mind. The essay "On Not Sleeping with Your Students" resonated with me the most and precisely articulated thoughts that at first seem abstract and controversial then self-evident. A grounded and captivating collection of essays that I would recommend to anyone interested in bringing intro feminist theory into daily life. Read more here.
